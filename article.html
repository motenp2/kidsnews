Fifth Graders Discover a New Way to Clean Plastic from Rivers Using Magnets

A group of young scientists at Riverside Elementary spent three months designing an experiment that could help remove microplastics from local waterways — and their results surprised even their teacher.

MJ
By Maya Johnson, Age 11 — Science Reporter
April 10, 2026 4 min read Riverside Elementary, Sacramento

It started with a science fair idea that most kids would have given up on after one failed experiment. But Maya Johnson and her four teammates at Riverside Elementary — ages 10 and 11 — kept going for three months, because they wanted to answer a question that mattered: can you pull microplastics out of water using a magnet?

The answer, after 47 trials and one very patient science teacher, turned out to be: yes. Sort of. And the "sort of" part is what makes their finding interesting.

What Are Microplastics, Anyway?

Before we get to the science, a quick explainer. Microplastics are tiny pieces of plastic — smaller than a grain of rice — that end up in rivers, oceans, and even the food we eat. Scientists are still studying how harmful they are, but most agree we'd be better off without them in our water.

"I learned about microplastics from a documentary my mom put on. I didn't sleep well that night. That's when I decided to do something about it."
— Maya Johnson, Age 11

The team's teacher, Ms. Elaine Ortega, says she was skeptical at first. "I told them it was ambitious. I didn't want them to get discouraged," she said. "But they proved me wrong in the best way."

How the Experiment Worked

The students mixed iron powder with tiny plastic beads in water — a simulated "microplastic solution." Then they coated the iron particles with a biodegradable polymer that helped the iron stick to the plastic. When they passed a strong magnet through the water, the iron-coated plastics were pulled out with it.

It took 23 tries to get the polymer coating right. Trial 24 was the breakthrough. "We saw the plastic clump together and go straight to the magnet," said team member Jordan Park. "We all screamed."

Their final test showed the method removed about 72% of the microplastics in a controlled sample. "That's not 100%," Maya acknowledged, "but real inventions don't start at 100%. They start somewhere."

What Happens Next

The team won first place at the district science fair and will present their work at the regional competition in May. A professor at UC Davis who heard about the project reached out and offered to let the team tour her environmental engineering lab this summer.

Ms. Ortega says what matters most isn't the prize. "These kids learned that big problems don't need adults to solve them. They just need curious people who refuse to quit."

Maya's advice to other young scientists? "Start with a problem that makes you angry. That's the fuel."
