Fifth Graders Discover a New Way to Clean Plastic from Rivers

By Leo Chen & Maya Rodriguez Updated April 12, 2026 8 MIN READ

It started with a simple question during recess: "Why is there so much glitter in the puddle?" For Maya Rodriguez and Leo Chen, two fifth graders at Sacramento Elementary, that question led to a three-month investigation into microplastics in local waterways.

Using 3D-printed filters and a custom-built centrifuge, the pair discovered a method to extract polyethylene fragments from river water with a 92% efficiency rate—surpassing some professional-grade municipal filters.

"We didn't think it would actually work," says Leo. "We just wanted to see if we could make the water look clearer. But when we looked under the microscope, the difference was huge."

The project has caught the attention of the City Council, which has invited the students to present their findings next month. For Maya, it's about more than just a science fair ribbon.

"People think kids just play," she says. "But we notice things that grownups ignore because they're too busy. We can see the glitter in the puddles."
